\section{Introduction}

   M-Dwarfs are among the most numerous stars in the universe, and it is suspected that many exist alongside at least one companion. Numerous attempts have been made to find the total fraction of M-Dwarfs which have any number of companions, but this multiplicity fraction is still not well constrained. Working towards knowing this value will have important and broad implications for star formation theories. \par
   Fundamentally, the multiplicity fraction of a population of stars depends on: 1. the companion mass ratio distribution and 2. the orbital surface density distribution. Using these two components, we calculate the multiplicity fraction as: \begin{equation}
    f = \int_{a_{min}}^{a_{max}}\phi dlog_{10}(a) * \int_{q_{min}}^{q_{max}}\psi dq \label{eq:1}
\end{equation} The first component, the companion mass ratio distribution, is well-described by Reggiani & Meyer (2013). They find the relationship: \begin{equation}
    \psi = q^{.25} \label{eq:2}
\end{equation}Our method for calculating the multiplicity fraction is built upon a key assumption: that the mass ratio distribution does not depend on orbital separation. Evidence for this is provided by Reggiani & Meyer 2013.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Acquiring the Data}
In order to explore the orbital surface density distribution and multiplicity of M-Dwarfs, our first step was to compile data from a variety of different multiplicity surveys: Cortes-Contreras et. al. (2016), Delfosse et. al. (1998), Fischer and Marcy (1992), Janson et. al. (2012), and Ward Duong et. al. (2015). This would later be used as point estimates of the multiplicity fraction for the purposes of fitting a model to the orbital surface density distribution. \par
   It was first necessary to have a firm understanding on the ability of each of these surveys to detect companions at various levels of mass ratio and semi-major axis in order to ensure the certainty of a companion detection. To this end, we sought to constrain companion detections to the values of mass ratio and semi-major axis which each survey was at least 90\% complete to. The mass ratio, q, is defined as: $\frac{M_{secondary}}{M_{primary}}$ where, by definition, $M_{primary} > M_{secondary}$ so that $q \leq 1$. Furthermore, the semi-major axis, a, of a system serves as a measure of the separation between the primary and secondary stars. We limited our analysis to the range of q which all of the referenced surveys were collectively sensitive to, [0.60 < q < 1.00], as it is important that each set of data be representative over the same range of q. In order to adequately investigate a broad range of semi-major axis, we utilized data from surveys which employed radial velocity (Delfosse et. al. (2016), Fischer and Marcy (1992)) and direct imaging (Cortes-Contreras et. al. , Janson et. al. (2012), and Ward Duong et. al. (2105)) methods. Radial velocity surveys are better able to detect close companions, while direct imaging surveys are sensitive to wide companions. Combining these sources allowed our analysis include data which is sensitive to a range of semi-major axis of [0.04 < a < 10,000 AU].\par
\subsection{Fitting the Model}
Next, we sought to fit a log-normal model to the orbital surface density distribution of the M-Dwarf multiple systems included in our study. This model, described by: \begin{equation}
   \phi = \frac{e^{-(log_{10}(a) - \mu)^{2} / (2\sigma^{2} )}}{\sigma*\sqrt{2\pi}} \label{eq:1}
\end{equation} was fit via the reduced chi-squared test for 3 free parameters: the base-10 log of mean ($log_{10}(\mu)$), the base-10 log of standard deviation ($log_{10}(\sigma)$), and amplitude (A). In order to fit the model, we made use of point estimates of the multiplicity fraction from the five M-Dwarf multiplicity surveys described above. The five surveys are collectively sensitive to mass ratios greater than 0.6 and orbital separations ranging from 0.04 to 10,000. Because it covers such a wide range of semi-major axis (3 - 10,000 AU), the data from Ward-Duong et. al. 2015 was split into two bins by orbital separation: 0 - 1,000 AU and 1,000 - 10,000 AU. This split is further justified by the fact that the survey utilized two different companion-confirmation methods for these two orbital separation regimes (archival plate analysis and adaptive optics imaging for the near and far separations respectively). This left 6 data points with which to fit 3 parameters, resulting in 3 degrees of freedom for the reduced chi-squared test. The model fit to this distribution allowed us to performs further calculation
\subsection{Calculating the Frequency}
Next, we used our model for the orbital surface density distribution alongside the companion mass ratio distribution model from Reggiani and Meyer 2013 to calculate the multiplicity of M-Dwarfs as: \begin{equation}
    f = \int_{a_{min}}^{a_{max}}\frac{e^{-(log_{10}(a) - \mu)^{2} / (2\sigma^{2} )}}{\sigma*\sqrt{2\pi}} dlog_{10}(a) * \int_{q_{min}}^{q_{max}}q^{.25} dq \label{eq:2}
\end{equation} This formula was first integrated over the ranges of q and a that encompass the survey data: 0.6 < q < 1.0 and 0.04 < a < 10,000 AU. This calculation resulted in a frequency that is representative over these limited ranges of q and a. These ranges were later expanded to 0.0 < q < 1.0 and 0 < a < 10\^10 AU to allow for the calculation of a universal M-Dwarf multiplicity fraction. The error on the multiplicity fraction was calculated as the 90\% confidence interval of the probability distribution function of the frequency.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Orbital Surface Density Model}
The best-fit to point estimates of frequency from the five M-Dwarf surveys resulted in the parameters of $log_{10}(\mu)$, $log_{10}(\sigma)$, and A being 1.582, 0.946, and 0.629 respectively. This fit returned a reduced chi-squared parameter value of 2.137. With 3 degrees of freedom, the chi-squared probability distribution indicates that the probability of achieving a value greater than or equal to 2.137 is 0.093. Despite this low probability, we do not reject the null hypothesis that the data came from this model because it exceeds the 0.05 significance level. The log normal model with the best-fit parameter values of $log_{10}(\mu)$, $log_{10}(\sigma)$, and A, shown below in Figure 1, describes the orbital surface density distribution of M-Dwarfs.\begin{figure}

\subsection{Multiplicity Fraction}
Integrating Equation 1 over the constrained regions of q (0.6 < q < 1) and a (0.04 < a < 10,000 AU) resulted in a multiplicity fraction of 0.236 +/- 0.061. Following this process, a universal multiplicity fraction was found by integrating Equation 1 over 0.0 < q < 1.0 and 0.0 < a < 10e10 AU to be 0.503 +/- 0.136.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{The Multiplicity of M-Dwarfs}
The results of our work suggest that around half of all M-Dwarfs have a companion. Because our study encompasses very small mass ratios, many of these companions may be Brown Dwarfs.
\subsection{Comparisons to Other Spectral Types}
We sought to compare the multiplicity of M-Dwarfs to that of the sun-like FGK and more massive A type stars over the constrained range of mass ratio and semi-major axis (0.6 < q < 1.0 and 0.04 < a < 10,000 AU). To compare to the FGK multiplicity, we extrapolated the model of orbital surface density from Raghavan et. al. 2010 to integrate over the above ranges of q and a. This, alongside the companion mass ratio distribution described in Equation 1, was used to find an FGK star multiplicity fraction of 0.230 +/- 0.032, where the error is estimated as the Poisson counting error based on the survey of Raghavan et. al. 2010. We used the same method for the A star multiplicity fraction, this time referencing De Rosa et. al. 2013, and found a multiplicity fraction of 0.238 +/- 0.026. In summary, we find the multiplicity fraction over 0.6 < q < 1.0 and 0.04 < a < 10,000 AU for M, FGK, and A stars to be 0.236 +/- 0.061, 0.230 +/- 0.032, and 0.238 +/- 0.026 respectively. We note that these three values are all within error of one-another, suggesting that the multiplicity fraction does not vary with spectral type. Future studies may explore the multiplicity fraction of OB type stars and Brown Dwarfs to see if this trend holds with the most massive stars and smaller sub-stellar objects.
